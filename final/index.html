Drug Overdoses in Connecticut

Embed from Getty Images

The buying and selling of illegal drugs in the United States has always had a large market. However, when most citizens think of the illegal drug trade in the U.S., most are quick to jump to lower-income or crime-stricken states and areas. Areas like the rural midwest or less fortunate regions of large cities come to mind. However, this broad generalization does not take into account the other side of the story: the side where people from wealthy, affluent areas turn to drugs due to their generally high income. The state of Connecticut has long held the reputation of being one of America’s wealthier states. In fact, the per capita income for Connecticut in 2022 was $78,510, putting it at the higher end of income in the United States. Namingly, Fairfield County and Tolland County see the highest amounts of income, making both extremely affluent areas known for having extremely wealthy residents. Fairfield County alone has a per capita income of $97,539, which is 50% higher than the national average.

One would think that the overwhelming amount of pure wealth in Fairfield County and the greater Connecticut area would protect the average citizen from the dangers of the illegal drug trade in the United States. However, there were 1,466 deaths from drug overdose-related causes throughout 2012 to 2021 in Fairfield county alone. When looking at all of Connecticut, though, there were a total 9,202 deaths from drug-overdose related causes. In 2021 alone, the state reached a record high of 1,524 deaths. Connecticut residents, according to the CDC, are more likely to die from a drug overdose than a motor-vehicle accident. In 2020, the rate for drug-induced mortality was 39.1 in Connecticut, compared to a national average of just 28.3. There has been a severe and alarming upward trend in drug use and overdoses in Connecticut, with a large portion of these deaths due to heroin or cocaine usage and intoxication. The number of drug-induced deaths has increased by 327% since 2012.

When looking at autopsies and toxicity reports, though, one might find it most alarming that most of the deaths result from a mixing of multiple drugs taken at one time- hinting at the fact that the drugs being sold and bought in Connecticut are not only illegal and unsafe to begin with, but also possibly laced with other dangerous substances unbeknownst to the customer. Xylazine, which is a known animal tranquilizer, was found in 20% of toxicity reports of Connecticut overdose deaths in 2021. This introduces an entirely new dynamic to the drug crisis facing the United States today, as there are even more aspects of danger to consider.

Additionally, the opioid crisis is not missed on Connecticut, either. Opioids continue to be one of the leading causes of death for drug abusers. In 2015, more than 60% of drug overdoses involved opioids. By 2021, that number had increased to a staggering 93% of drug overdose cases involving some kind of opioid, with the most common being fentanyl. The major issue with the opioid crisis is that the drugs are extremely accessible to the average citizen. 60% of overdose deaths occurred in residences, as opposed to hospitals or other locations. Unfortunately, modern-day medicine and prescriptions essentially put opioids in the hands of abusers, if not handled correctly by the citizen legally receiving the medicine.

Suzanne Harrison, a member of the Emergency Medical Services in New Canaan, explained the strong correlation between opiates and overdoses. “[Opiates] are so highly addictive and people generally start out on them as pain meds then become dependent”, Harrison said. She also explains that opiates start out as a gateway drug for many, as the users “go from getting a prescription to buying pills on the street to shooting heroin because it’s so much cheaper”, she said.

Drug Overdoses in Connecticut: The Demographics

According to the CDC, the third wave of overdose deaths in the United States is largely due to the illegal manufacturing of synthetic opioids. Most of these dangerous and not FDA-approved drugs contain fentanyl, which makes its way into a lot of the cases of Connecticut overdoses. Harrison explains, “The biggest threat for young adults using drugs is that almost everything is being made with the synthetic opiate Fentanyl because it’s so much cheaper”. Harrison interprets the sharp rise in drug use by young adults is in part due to “sports-related and other injuries being treated with pain meds”, which gives these adolescents a high accessibility to getting ahold of opiates. This may lead to an even larger population of drug abusers in Connecticut, as these young adults might decide to continue this behavior into adulthood.

Drug-induced deaths in Connecticut are spiking at an alarming rate, affecting every county and demographic of citizens. However, it is not only Connecticut facing this hard reality. The entire United States is currently seeing an all-time high of drug overdoses and other abusive behaviors, and it is quickly becoming a full-fledged crisis for all American citizens. According to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, the opioid crisis was declared a public health emergency in 2017, and the HSS is still working on plans and strategies to attempt to combat this dangerous struggle. Because of the accessibility of these drugs, however, the epidemic is extremely hard to fight. To prevent proliferating this emergency, when prescribed opiates or other painkillers, one should always remember to immediately discard the excess pills after use.
